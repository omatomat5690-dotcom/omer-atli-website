There is a curious gap between how much noise vitamin D generates online and how simple the actual UK public-health advice is. Wellness culture has turned it into a near-mystical cure-all, sold in doses that would make a 1970s endocrinologist wince. Meanwhile the official recommendation — the one from the body that actually reviewed the evidence — fits in a sentence and asks for a modest tablet. This article is about that recommendation: what the Scientific Advisory Committee on Nutrition genuinely advises, why the British winter is the reason it exists, and where the megadose mythology parts company with the science.

## What does SACN actually advise?

In plain terms: everyone in the UK should consider taking a daily supplement containing 10 micrograms of vitamin D during the autumn and winter months. Some groups are advised to take it all year round.

SACN — the Scientific Advisory Committee on Nutrition — is the independent expert body that advises UK government on diet and health. When it reviewed vitamin D, it landed on a deliberately unglamorous figure: 10 micrograms (that's 400 international units) a day for the general population over autumn and winter. The number is not a wellness-influencer's "optimising" dose; it is a public-health recommendation aimed at keeping the population's vitamin D status above the level at which bone and muscle problems start to appear.

## Why the winter, specifically?

Because in the UK, for roughly half the year, your skin simply cannot make the stuff. We produce most of our vitamin D in the skin from sunlight, and from about October to March the sun sits too low in British latitudes for the right wavelengths to reach us. The skin's chemistry effectively switches off.

This isn't a theory or a marketing hook — it's geography. Through spring and summer, most people with reasonable outdoor exposure make enough to see them through. As the days shorten, that supply tails off, and dietary sources rarely fill the gap on their own. That seasonal shutdown is the entire logic of the autumn-and-winter advice: it targets the months when the natural supply line is closed.

## Who is at risk all year round?

Some people don't get the summer top-up in the first place, and for them SACN advises a supplement throughout the year — not just in winter.

The year-round groups are those whose skin synthesis is limited regardless of season, or whose needs are higher. They include people who spend little time outdoors or are housebound; those who habitually cover most of their skin when outside; and people with darker skin tones, in whom more melanin reduces the amount of vitamin D the skin makes from a given dose of sunlight. Infants and young children, and pregnant and breastfeeding women, have their own specific NHS recommendations. The common thread is the same: where the summer supply can't be relied upon, the supplement runs all year.

## Is routine testing necessary?

For most people, no. Vitamin D is not a level that needs checking routinely in someone who is well. The advice is built around a sensible standing supplement, not around testing everyone and chasing a number.

Routine blood testing of vitamin D in healthy people is generally not recommended — it adds cost and anxiety without changing the simple, safe action most people should take anyway. Testing has its place when there's a clinical reason: symptoms or conditions that point towards deficiency, or specific risk factors a clinician wants to investigate. But the public-health model deliberately sidesteps mass testing in favour of a modest, low-risk supplement. If you think you might be deficient because of symptoms, that's a conversation with your GP, not a reason to order a home kit and self-interpret the result.

## The megadose myths

Here is where online vitamin D culture goes off the rails. The marketing logic runs: if a little is good for your bones, a lot must be better for everything. The evidence does not support either half of that sentence.

On the "better for everything" side: vitamin D has been studied, hopefully and repeatedly, as a near-universal protector — against cancer, heart disease, infections, low mood. Large randomised trials, including the substantial VITAL study, were mostly deflating. Supplementing people who weren't deficient did not meaningfully shift those outcomes. Vitamin D corrects deficiency, which genuinely matters in a sun-starved country; it is not a tonic that improves an already-replete body.

On the "more is better" side: it is actively untrue. Vitamin D is fat-soluble, so the body stores it, and very high doses over time can push blood calcium up to harmful levels — a state called hypercalcaemia, which can affect the kidneys and heart. This is exactly why SACN also sets an upper limit for routine intake well below the eye-watering doses sold online. The 10-microgram recommendation isn't timidity; it's the dose that does the job without courting harm.

## What about food?

Diet alone rarely meets the winter need, but a few foods help. Oily fish — salmon, mackerel, sardines — is the standout natural source. Egg yolks and red meat contribute smaller amounts, and some foods in the UK, such as certain breakfast cereals and fat spreads, are fortified. For most people through autumn and winter, food nudges things along but doesn't replace the supplement, which is precisely why the supplement is advised rather than "just eat more fish".

## Practical takeaways

- SACN advises everyone in the UK consider a 10-microgram (400 IU) daily vitamin D supplement during autumn and winter, when the skin can't make it from sunlight.
- People with limited sun exposure, those who cover their skin, and people with darker skin are advised to supplement all year round.
- Routine vitamin D testing isn't needed for healthy people; testing is for when there's a clinical reason, decided with a clinician.
- More is not better: very high doses can raise calcium to harmful levels, and big trials show no broad benefit from supplementing people who aren't deficient.
- Oily fish, egg yolks and fortified foods help, but rarely cover the winter gap on their own.

## What this doesn't mean

It doesn't mean vitamin D is unimportant — genuine deficiency causes real harm to bones and muscle, and a winter supplement is a sensible, low-cost thing for most people in the UK to take. It does mean the "more is better, fixes everything" framing is wrong on both counts. The recommendation is modest on purpose.

## When to seek medical advice

If you have symptoms you're attributing to deficiency — persistent bone pain, muscle weakness, marked fatigue — see your GP rather than self-diagnosing, because those symptoms have many causes. Speak to your GP or pharmacist before taking high-dose vitamin D, especially if you have kidney problems, take other supplements containing calcium, or are pregnant, as upper limits and interactions matter more than the packaging suggests.

## A closing thought

Vitamin D is a rare case where the official advice is both genuinely useful and genuinely boring — a small winter tablet, no testing, no heroics. The internet finds that unsatisfying and reaches for the megadose instead, as if the size of the capsule were a measure of how much you care about your health. The science says the opposite: the modest dose does the real work, and the impressive-looking one mostly courts the harm. Boring, here, is exactly right.
